# CTRL+WATCH — Boss Fight Skill

## What a Boss Fight Is

Two channels. Same broad territory. One winner declared — no draws, no hedging.

The Boss Fight is not a tie-breaker or a ranking exercise. It's a question: *if you could only watch one of these channels forever, which one would it be and why?* The answer must be argued, not calculated.

Total length: **1,200–1,800 words** plus dual scorecard.

---

## Continuity Check — Used Matchups

Never repeat a pairing. **Read `.claude/tracker.md` → "Boss Fight — Used Matchups"** before selecting any matchup. The tracker has the complete list of used matchups, used channels, and pre-approved safe matchups.

---

## Matchup Selection Criteria

A good Boss Fight requires:

1. **Same territory, different approach** — two channels doing recognisably similar things in different ways. Not two channels that happen to share a genre.
2. **Both channels have genuine merit** — Boss Fights are not punching bags. The loser should be someone worth losing to.
3. **A real argument to be made** — if the winner is obvious, the fight is boring. The best matchups are ones where the magazine could be wrong.
4. **Theme connection** — the matchup should illuminate something about the issue's theme, not just fill the section.

**Avoid:**
- Mismatched fights (a 90-scored channel vs a 70-scored channel — the result is predetermined)
- Matchups where one channel has dramatically more output (unfair comparison)
- Repeating a channel that has already been in a Boss Fight (check tracker)

## Structure

### 1. Title Card
```
⚔ BOSS FIGHT ⚔
[CHANNEL A] vs [CHANNEL B]
Category: [What They're Competing In — e.g., "History Comedy", "Music Theory"]
```

### 2. Introduction (2–3 paragraphs)
Why does this matchup matter? What does it reveal about YouTube, the issue theme, or the genre? This is not just setup — it's the argument the fight will prove or disprove. Don't just say "both channels are great." Say what's at stake.

### 3. Tale of the Tape
Quick-reference stats comparison. Presented as a two-column table or visual layout.

```
┌────────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────┐
│       [CHANNEL A]          │       [CHANNEL B]          │
├────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
│ Est: [year]                │ Est: [year]                │
│ Subs: ~[X]M                │ Subs: ~[X]M                │
│ Output: [frequency]        │ Output: [frequency]        │
│ Format: [description]      │ Format: [description]      │
│ Best known for: [video]    │ Best known for: [video]    │
│ Weakness: [honest]         │ Weakness: [honest]         │
└────────────────────────────┴────────────────────────────┘
```

The "Weakness" field is mandatory. It prevents the Tale of the Tape from becoming a PR sheet.

### 4. Five Rounds
One round per scoring category. Each round: 150–250 words. Declare a round winner.

**Round structure:**
- How does Channel A approach this category?
- How does Channel B approach this category?
- Where they differ specifically
- Round verdict: [CHANNEL A WINS] / [CHANNEL B WINS] / [DRAW — allowed in rounds only]

**Category order:**
1. Content Quality
2. Consistency  
3. Replay Value
4. Community
5. X-Factor

**X-Factor round is the most important.** It should be the longest and the most argued. The X-Factor round often decides the fight.

### 5. Dual Scorecard

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                FINAL SCORECARD                                  │
├──────────────────────┬──────────────┬──────────────────────────┤
│ Category             │ [CHANNEL A]  │ [CHANNEL B]              │
├──────────────────────┼──────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
│ Content Quality      │ XX           │ XX                       │
│ Consistency          │ XX           │ XX                       │
│ Replay Value         │ XX           │ XX                       │
│ Community            │ XX           │ XX                       │
│ X-Factor             │ XX           │ XX                       │
├──────────────────────┼──────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
│ OVERALL              │ XX           │ XX                       │
└──────────────────────┴──────────────┴──────────────────────────┘
```

### 6. The Decision (2–3 paragraphs)
Declare the winner. This is not a summary — it's the argument's conclusion. Why does this channel win, specifically? What does winning mean in this context? What does the loser do that the winner can't?

The Decision should acknowledge the loser's best quality genuinely, then explain why it isn't enough.

### 7. Post-Fight (1 paragraph)
Top 50 implications. Does either channel enter, move, or get displaced? Does the winner earn a re-evaluation? Brief, factual.

## Scoring Guidance for Boss Fights

Boss Fight scores are independent of but consistent with Player Profile scores. A channel scored in a Boss Fight:
- May have been reviewed before (use prior score as reference — don't contradict it without reason)
- May not have been reviewed before (Boss Fight is their first formal score)
- Scores entered in Boss Fights count as official channel scores for Top 50 purposes

**Score drift rule:** A Boss Fight score should not differ from a prior Player Profile score by more than 5 points without editorial justification noted in the Decision section.

---

## The Editorial Override

When scores are close (within 3–5 points overall), the winner is not automatically the higher scorer. The editor can declare a winner on qualitative grounds — but must argue the case explicitly.

**When override is appropriate:**
- The higher-scoring channel wins on metrics but the lower-scoring channel is more irreplaceable
- X-Factor is significantly better on one side despite other categories being even
- One channel's weakness is in a category that the issue theme specifically values

**Override language:**
> "On the scorecard, [Channel A] has it by [X] points. But Boss Fights aren't scored on points. [Channel B] does something that [Channel A] cannot: [specific claim]. That's why [Channel B] wins."

The override must be used sparingly. If every close fight gets overridden, the scoring system loses meaning.

---

## Tone Calibration

**Both channels must be respected.** The loser of a Boss Fight is still worth watching. This is not a negative review — it's a comparison.

**The loser should feel honoured to have lost here.**

Common tone failure: the losing channel's review in the fight reads like a takedown. Fix: reread the loser's round descriptions and make sure each one acknowledges genuine strength before finding the limits.

**Avoid:**
- "Channel A is clearly better" — if it's that clear, the fight is boring
- Deciding the winner before writing the rounds (let the rounds discover the answer)
- Pretending the result surprised you when it didn't
- Generic praise ("both channels are great examples of...") — be specific

---

## Boss Fight and the Top 50

After every Boss Fight, update the Top 50:
- Winner: enters or moves up if score justifies
- Loser: enters or holds position based on score
- Neither channel is penalised for losing — the fight establishes their score, not their worth

**Check `.claude/tracker.md` → "Top 50"** for current rankings before and after writing any Boss Fight.

## Quality Check

Before finalising any Boss Fight:

- [ ] Matchup not used before (check continuity tracker)
- [ ] Neither channel reviewed in a previous Boss Fight (check tracker)
- [ ] Introduction argues a real thesis — not just setup
- [ ] Tale of the Tape includes honest weakness for both channels
- [ ] Five rounds completed — all categories covered
- [ ] At least one draw in the rounds (makes the fight feel real)
- [ ] X-Factor round is the longest and most argued
- [ ] Dual scorecard complete with all five categories
- [ ] Winner declared — no hedging, no draws at overall level
- [ ] Editorial override (if used) argued explicitly
- [ ] Loser acknowledged genuinely — not as a consolation, as a real point
- [ ] Top 50 implications noted in Post-Fight section
- [ ] Scores consistent with any prior Player Profile scores for these channels
