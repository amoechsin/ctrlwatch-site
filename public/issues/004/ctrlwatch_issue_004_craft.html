CTRL+WATCH
ISSUE #004 // FEBRUARY 2026
CRAFT

PRESS START

By the Editor

I've been thinking about hands.

Not in a weird way. In a craft way. The way Stanley Kubrick's hands moved a camera. The way Julia Child's hands broke down a chicken. The way Bob Ross's hands danced across canvas with a palette knife. Hands that made things, not just performed things.

YouTube has a craft problem. Not a content problem. Not even a quality problem, strictly speaking. A craft problem.

Somewhere between the invention of the thumbnail A/B test and the discovery that human faces expressing shock drive clicks, we stopped asking "how is this made?" and started asking "how does this perform?" The result is an ocean of content that looks professional, sounds professional, hits all the algorithmic checkboxes, and contains absolutely zero evidence that a human being spent time making it with care.

This issue is about the opposite of that. It's about creators who give a damn about how things are made. Who understand that technique matters. That detail matters. That the difference between good and great often lives in the hundredth iteration, the thing nobody notices but everybody feels.

"Craft is caring about the things other people don't notice until they're missing."

We sat down with six departed masters for our Time Capsule interviews this month. Stanley Kubrick on YouTube filmmaking. Julia Child on cooking content. Bob Ross on art tutorials. Jim Henson on creative channels. Anthony Bourdain on food YouTube. Roger Ebert on video criticism. None of them made content for algorithms. All of them changed their mediums through sheer craft excellence.

Their reactions to modern YouTube are... educational.

In our reviews, we're looking at channels that take craft seriously. Technology Connections, where every frame is considered. Every Frame a Painting, the channel that redefined video essays through meticulous construction. Primitive Technology, where less is spectacularly more. And yes, we're also reviewing 5-Minute Crafts, because sometimes you need to examine what happens when craft is replaced entirely by content formula and hot glue.

Our Boss Fight this month: Primitive Technology versus How To Make Everything. Both channels about making things from scratch. Both deeply committed to showing process. One winner. The decision might surprise you.

We've also expanded Hidden Levels this issue with a special focus on craft channels under 10K subscribers. These are the makers, the builders, the obsessives who spend six months hand-crafting a 12-minute video about how to properly sharpen a chisel. The algorithm doesn't love them. We do.

Fair warning: this issue is going to make you want to learn a skill. Any skill. Woodworking. Cooking. Film editing. Anything that requires you to develop technique, to practice, to give a damn about details. YouTube's greatest trick was convincing everyone that being interesting is enough. It's not. Being good at something is what lasts.

One last thing. This issue features the most opinionated Yob responses we've ever run. Readers wrote in asking about everything from whether expensive gear matters (it doesn't, mostly) to whether YouTube has killed genuine craftsmanship (jury's out). Yob was... Yob. You'll see.

Craft isn't nostalgic. It's not retro. It's not a rebellion against modern content. It's simply the practice of giving a damn about how things are made, why they work, and whether they're built to last. In a medium that refreshes every 0.3 seconds, that might be the most radical thing you can do.

Now stop reading editorials and go learn how to do something properly.

— The Editor

NOW LOADING

MrBeast Launches "CraftBeast" Series, Misses Point Entirely
In news that surprised absolutely nobody, MrBeast announced a new series focusing on "traditional craftsmanship" that involves giving woodworkers $100,000 to make chairs in 24 hours. When asked whether he understood that craft requires time, patience, and iteration, his team reportedly replied "time is money though." The first video featured a master furniture maker looking physically pained as he was asked to speedrun a Windsor chair for content. Yob's Take: "Mate, this is like asking Gordon Ramsay to make beef wellington in a microwave. Technically possible, completely missing the point. 1/5 stars."
Technology Connections Hits 3M Subscribers, Refuses to Change Anything
Alec Watson's channel Technology Connections crossed three million subscribers this month while steadfastly maintaining his commitment to 20-minute videos about how thermostats work. In a move that baffled growth consultants everywhere, he celebrated by... uploading a 45-minute video about heat pumps. "Why would I change what works?" he told nobody, because he doesn't do interviews. The video immediately hit #1 on Trending. Sometimes craft wins. Sometimes.
Primitive Technology Returns After Two-Year Hiatus, Says Nothing
John Plant uploaded his first video in 27 months. No announcement. No explanation. No apology tour. Just "Making a Water-Powered Hammer" with zero narration and subtitles enabled. It immediately earned 8 million views and reminded everyone what actual craft content looks like. The comment section was 90% "the king returns" and 10% people asking if he's okay. He has not responded. He will not respond. This is the way.
5-Minute Crafts Launches "Master Crafts" Channel, Internet Laughs
The content farm behind 5-Minute Crafts announced a premium channel called "Master Crafts" featuring "expert-level DIY projects." The first video showed someone making a coffee table out of popsicle sticks and E6000 adhesive. When questioned about structural integrity, a spokesperson said "it's about the vibe." The table collapsed during filming. They kept it in. 187 million views in three days. We are living in a simulation.
Every Frame a Painting Archive Passes 100 Million Total Views
Tony Zhou's legendary video essay channel, inactive since 2016, quietly passed 100 million lifetime views this month. The channel hasn't uploaded in eight years. The videos are still required viewing for anyone who wants to understand visual storytelling. Meanwhile, 47 video essay channels have launched this week alone, most of them using the exact editing style Tony pioneered, none of them crediting him. Craft respects craft. Content just takes.

TIME CAPSULE

Where we resurrect the departed to witness the chaos they missed.

STANLEY KUBRICK ON YOUTUBE FILMMAKING

SATIRICAL / FICTIONAL — Stanley Kubrick (1928-1999) did not participate in this Q&A.
C+W: Mr. Kubrick, thank you for—
KUBRICK: [interrupting] How many takes did you do to get that question right?
C+W: I... this is print. There are no takes.
KUBRICK: Exactly the problem. [lights cigarette] You're asking me about YouTube. A platform where people upload videos in one take. Where "good enough" is the standard. Where the edit is automated. [long pause] Do you understand what you're asking me to evaluate?
C+W: Some YouTube filmmakers do dozens of takes—
KUBRICK: And then they compress the footage to save bandwidth. They export at bitrates I wouldn't use for a VHS demo reel. They let an algorithm crop the frame. [leans forward] Let me tell you something about craft. It's not just doing things carefully. It's controlling every variable. Every. Single. One. The light. The lens. The film stock. The lab timing. The projection. YouTube filmmakers don't control the projection. They don't even control the aspect ratio. How can you call that filmmaking?
C+W: But some YouTube creators do exceptional visual work. Channels like Film Courage, Cinematography Database—
KUBRICK: [almost smiling] Oh, I've seen some things. That corridor tracking shot from that gamer channel... what was it? Corridor Digital? The de-aging technology video? [pauses] Competent. Very competent. They understand composition. They understand lighting. But do you know what they don't understand?
C+W: What's that?
KUBRICK: Patience. Real filmmaking is waiting three days for the right weather. It's building a set because location isn't perfect. It's doing 127 takes because 126 weren't right. YouTube is "we're losing daylight" and "we'll fix it in post" and "the algorithm wants uploads every Tuesday." [exhales smoke] You can't rush craft. The moment you let external pressure—commercial, temporal, algorithmic—dictate your process, you've already compromised.
C+W: But the accessibility argument—anyone can make films now—
KUBRICK: Anyone can make videos. There's a difference. [stands, walks to window] In my day, the barrier to entry was high. You needed money, equipment, distribution. That was a problem. But it also meant that people who cleared that barrier were committed. They'd spent years learning the craft. Now? People shoot a wedding, post it on YouTube, call themselves filmmakers. [turns back] Democratization of tools doesn't equal democratization of skill.
C+W: There's a creator called Tom Scott who spent months planning single videos—
KUBRICK: [interested] Go on.
C+W: He choreographs everything. Multiple locations. Complex logistics. He once spent a year setting up a single shot involving a moving train. The craft is meticulous.
KUBRICK: [nods slowly] That's filmmaking. That's the difference. It's not about the platform. It's about whether you respect the process enough to serve it properly. [sits back down] Here's what YouTube has destroyed: the ability to fail privately. In film school, you make terrible shorts. You learn. You improve. Nobody sees the failures. On YouTube, everything is public. Everything is permanent. So people optimize for "not embarrassing" instead of "excellent." They play it safe. Safe is the death of craft.
C+W: Some creators have moved to Patreon specifically to escape that pressure—
KUBRICK: Good. Let them. The best work happens when you remove the audience from the process. Not from the result—the result is always for the audience. But from the process. [stubs out cigarette] You want to know the real problem with YouTube filmmaking? It's not the compression. It's not the algorithm. It's that creators know how the video performs before they've even finished watching it themselves. They see the analytics. The retention graphs. The click-through rates. That data changes how you think about your own work. It's poison.
C+W: So YouTube can't support real filmmaking?
KUBRICK: [long pause] I didn't say that. YouTube can support real filmmaking the moment a creator decides the work matters more than the metrics. Upload it and walk away. Don't look at the analytics for a month. Make something because it has to exist, not because it might perform. [lights another cigarette] The platform doesn't make the filmmaker. The filmmaker makes the film. Everything else is just... distribution.
C+W: Last question. If you were starting out today as a filmmaker, would you use YouTube?
KUBRICK: [slight smile] Absolutely not. I'd use it as a research tool. Study every frame of every great film ever made. Then I'd shoot on the highest quality medium I could access, edit without reference to anyone's opinion, and release it when it was finished. Not when it was due. When it was finished. And if that takes three years? [shrugs] Then it takes three years. Craft doesn't have a content calendar.

JULIA CHILD ON COOKING YOUTUBE

SATIRICAL / FICTIONAL — Julia Child (1912-2004) did not participate in this Q&A.
C+W: Mrs. Child, welcome back. We'd love to talk about cooking content on YouTube—
JULIA: [delighted] Oh wonderful! I've been watching! It's absolutely marvelous how many people are cooking now! Though I must say, some of them are doing very peculiar things with butter.
C+W: Peculiar how?
JULIA: Not using enough! [laughs, that distinctive warble] I watched a young woman make beurre blanc with—I counted—exactly one tablespoon of butter. One tablespoon! For a sauce! I wanted to reach through the screen and say "Darling, you're making grey water, not beurre blanc!" [waves hand] But then I realized she was worried about the comments. People calling it "unhealthy." Can you imagine? Worrying about what strangers think of your butter ratio?
C+W: The health-conscious approach to cooking content has definitely changed things—
JULIA: It's killed the joy! [emphatic] Cooking is not about optimization. It's not about macros or meal prep or "clean eating." It's about transformation. Taking raw ingredients and making something delicious. Something that makes people happy. These "healthy" cooking channels with their cauliflower rice and their nutritional yeast— [shudders theatrically] —they're teaching people to be afraid of food. That's not craft. That's anxiety with a whisk.
C+W: But there are channels teaching classical technique. Bon Appétit, for example—
JULIA: [brightening] Oh yes! The test kitchen videos! I adore those! That lovely woman Claire—the one who makes gourmet versions of candy bars—she understands process. She shows the failures! [claps hands] That's the key! The failures! When I dropped that chicken on the floor during the pilot, the producer wanted to cut it. I said absolutely not. People need to see that cooking is messy. It's imperfect. You adapt. That's the craft.
C+W: Speaking of process, there's a trend called "30-second recipes"—
JULIA: [pause] Thirty... seconds?
C+W: Vertical videos. Very fast cuts. Just showing the key steps—
JULIA: But cooking is the steps between the steps! It's the stirring! The waiting! Understanding when the onions go from translucent to properly caramelized! [leans forward intensely] You cannot teach craft in thirty seconds. You can teach a trick. You can teach a hack. But craft requires time. It requires watching the process unfold. [sits back] These videos are recipes without cooking. They're IKEA instructions for food.
C+W: What about the production quality? These videos have perfect lighting, multiple cameras—
JULIA: [dismissive wave] Oh who cares? We shot French Chef on a public television budget with one camera and lights that made the kitchen unbearably hot. The quality that matters is whether you can see what's happening in the pan. Can you see when the butter foams? Can you see the texture change when you fold the egg whites? That's the only production value that matters. Everything else is just... [searching for word] ...prettiness.
C+W: There's a creator named Adam Ragusea who does incredibly detailed technique videos. Very educational, very precise—
JULIA: [nodding enthusiastically] Yes! Yes! I've watched his pizza dough video four times! He explains the why! Why you knead. Why you rest. Why you use cold fermentation. That's teaching craft. Not just "do this and it'll work." He's teaching people to understand their ingredients. To develop intuition. [voice softening] That's what we tried to do on The French Chef. Make people comfortable enough to experiment. To fail. To try again.
C+W: But YouTube cooking has also created this other trend—the "aesthetic" cooking video. No talking, just beautiful shots of food being prepared to music—
JULIA: [thoughtful] I've seen those. They're lovely to look at. But they don't teach anything. They're food pornography, not food education. [quick smile] Though I will say, watching someone make fresh pasta in a sun-drenched Italian kitchen is rather soothing. I'm not immune to beauty! But it's not craft. It's performance of craft. There's a difference.
C+W: What would you say to young creators who want to make cooking content that actually teaches?
JULIA: [direct, serious] Show the whole process. Don't cut out the boring parts because the boring parts are where craft lives. Talk while you cook—explain what you're thinking. If something goes wrong, show it. Teach people that cooking is a conversation with ingredients, not a script. And for heaven's sake— [grins] —use enough butter. The algorithm will survive. Your sauce won't.
C+W: Last question: If you were starting The French Chef today, on YouTube, what would you do differently?
JULIA: [immediate response] Absolutely nothing. Same format. Same approach. Teach technique, show the process, don't worry about the mistakes. The medium doesn't matter. The craft does. And if the algorithm doesn't like it? [magnificent shrug] Well! The algorithm doesn't need to eat the boeuf bourguignon, does it?

BOB ROSS ON ART TUTORIAL YOUTUBE

SATIRICAL / FICTIONAL — Bob Ross (1942-1995) did not participate in this Q&A.
C+W: Bob, thank you for joining us. We wanted to talk about art education on YouTube—
BOB: [soft voice, gentle smile] Well now, that sounds just wonderful. You know, I think it's amazing that anyone with a camera and some paints can share their joy with the world. That's what it's all about—sharing the joy.
C+W: YouTube has thousands of art tutorial channels now. Some teaching classical techniques, some teaching digital art—
BOB: [nodding] I've watched some of those digital art videos. Remarkable what people can do with a computer. Though I must say... [gentle pause] ...I do miss the happy accidents. When you're painting on a screen and you don't like something, you just... [makes erasing gesture] ...undo it. Ctrl-Z, I think they call it. [quieter] But some of my favorite paintings came from accidents. From mistakes that turned into mountains or trees or birds.
C+W: The "undo" function has definitely changed how people approach making art—
BOB: It's taught people to be afraid. [still gentle, but firm] That's the opposite of what art should do. Art should teach you that mistakes are just opportunities. That there are no mistakes, only happy accidents. When you can erase everything instantly... [shakes head] ...you never learn to work with what you've got. You never learn to adapt. That's a shame.
C+W: There's a huge following for "speedpaint" videos—artists recording themselves drawing or painting, then speeding it up—
BOB: [chuckles] Oh my. [thoughtful] I suppose that's interesting to watch, but what are people learning? They're seeing a finished piece appear like magic. They're not seeing the decision-making. The why of each stroke. [taps brush against easel] When we did The Joy of Painting, we painted in real time. Twenty-six minutes, start to finish. People could paint along. They could make the same decisions at the same pace. That's teaching. Speedpaints are just... entertainment.
C+W: Some creators argue they're inspiring people to try art themselves—
BOB: [immediate warmth] Oh, inspiration is wonderful. If even one person picks up a brush because they saw a beautiful speedpaint, that's a good thing. That's a very good thing. [pause] But I wonder... [measuring words carefully] ...if they're inspired to make art, or inspired to make art that looks like that. There's a difference. When someone watches a speedpaint of some hyperrealistic portrait, do they think "I want to paint," or do they think "I could never do that"?
C+W: That's a real problem in art tutorial spaces—people getting discouraged by comparing their work to professionals—
BOB: And YouTube makes it worse. [still gentle, but sad] Because everyone's showing their best work. Their finished pieces. Their most impressive techniques. Nobody's showing the painting they did yesterday that didn't quite work out. Nobody's showing the sketch that went wrong. So beginners think that everyone makes perfect art except them. [leans forward] That's not true. That's never been true. But YouTube makes it feel true.
C+W: There are some channels trying to change that. Channels that show the full process, mistakes and all—
BOB: [brightening] Those are the ones doing it right. Those are the ones teaching craft, not just showing off. [picks up brush] You know, when we started The Joy of Painting, the whole concept was that anyone could do this. Not anyone could make perfect art, but anyone could make their art. Could make something that brought them joy. That's what craft is. It's not perfection. It's practice with purpose.
C+W: What about the critique that your wet-on-wet technique, while accessible, doesn't teach "serious" painting skills—
BOB: [soft laugh] Well, I've heard that before. And you know what? They're right. Wet-on-wet isn't how you paint a masterpiece for a museum. [meets eyes directly] But it is how you teach someone that painting doesn't have to be scary. That you can make something beautiful in half an hour. And once they know that... [gentle shrug] ...once they believe they can do it, then maybe they try other techniques. Maybe they take a class. Maybe they develop their own style. You have to start somewhere. Wet-on-wet is a good start.
C+W: YouTube has made art education incredibly accessible—free tutorials, technique breakdowns, material reviews—
BOB: That's wonderful. That's truly wonderful. When I was learning to paint, I had to find books. Had to take classes. Had to learn from other painters when I could. Now? [gestures expansively] You can learn anything. Any medium. Any style. Any technique. That's a gift. [quieter] The challenge is not getting lost. There's so much information that people don't know where to start. They watch 50 tutorials and never pick up a brush because they're trying to learn everything before they begin. That's backwards.
C+W: What would you tell someone just starting to watch art tutorials on YouTube?
BOB: [direct, warm] Watch one video. Then stop watching and start painting. Doesn't matter if it's perfect. Doesn't matter if it looks like the tutorial. What matters is that you tried. That you made something. [pause] Craft isn't learned by watching. It's learned by doing. The tutorials are there to guide you, not to replace the doing. So watch a little, paint a lot. Make happy accidents. Make mistakes. Then make something beautiful.
C+W: If you were starting The Joy of Painting today, on YouTube instead of PBS—
BOB: [immediate smile] Same show. Same format. Same happy little trees. [softer] The platform doesn't matter. What matters is whether you're helping people find their joy. Whether you're teaching them that art isn't about being perfect—it's about being present. Making something with your hands. Creating something that didn't exist before. [looks at easel] That's craft. And that's the same whether it's 1983 or 2026. Now... [picks up brush] ...let's paint some happy little clouds.

JIM HENSON ON YOUTUBE CREATIVITY

SATIRICAL / FICTIONAL — Jim Henson (1936-1990) did not participate in this Q&A.
C+W: Jim, you pioneered puppetry on television, pushed the boundaries of what children's programming could be—
JIM: [gentle interruption, smiling] We just wanted to do something that didn't exist yet. That's all. Make people laugh. Make them think. Show them something they hadn't seen before.
C+W: YouTube has enabled that same kind of experimentation—creators can try anything—
JIM: Can they? [thoughtful, not confrontational] Or are they experimenting within a very specific set of constraints? Video length that the algorithm likes. Thumbnails that drive clicks. Content that won't get demonetized. [leans back] When we made The Muppet Show, we had constraints too—budget, time, broadcast standards. But we didn't have an algorithm telling us that episodes with musical guests performed 23% better than episodes with comedians.
C+W: You don't think YouTube enables genuine creativity?
JIM: I didn't say that. [warm smile] I've seen incredible creativity on YouTube. I've seen people doing things with puppetry that make me wish I had another lifetime to explore. Stop-motion animation channels. Practical effects tutorials. People building entire fantasy worlds in their garages. [pause] But I've also seen what happens when creativity meets metrics. When artists start making decisions based on what performs rather than what excites them. That's when craft dies.
C+W: There's a creator named Worthikids who does surreal, hand-crafted animation—
JIM: [immediately engaged] Oh! The wire-frame style animation? With the absurdist humor? That's exactly what I'm talking about. That's someone making things because they have to make them. Because the vision in their head demands to exist. [gestures enthusiastically] You can see the handmade quality. The roughness. The personality. It's not polished to algorithmic perfection. It's alive.
C+W: But channels like that often struggle to find audiences compared to more... optimized content—
JIM: [nodding seriously] That's the tragedy of YouTube. The platform can host anything, but it amplifies sameness. It rewards content that fits the pattern. [voice quieter] When we pitched The Muppet Show, every network in America rejected it. Said it was too weird. Too experimental. Said adults wouldn't watch puppets. We had to sell it to British television. [slight smile] It became the most-watched show in the world. Sometimes the weird thing is the right thing. But you have to be willing to make it anyway.
C+W: YouTube has this tension between craft and commercial success—
JIM: Every creative medium has that tension. Television had it. Film has it. Publishing has it. [leans forward] The difference with YouTube is the speed of feedback. In television, you made a show, it aired, you heard reactions later. On YouTube, you upload a video and within hours you know exactly how it performed. Views. Likes. Comments. Revenue. [shakes head] That immediate feedback changes how you think. It's very hard to stay true to your creative vision when you're watching the analytics in real-time.
C+W: Some creators have deliberately turned off analytics to avoid that influence—
JIM: Good for them. [genuine approval] That takes courage. Because they're giving up information that could help them "succeed" according to the platform's definition of success. But success isn't views. Success is making the thing you wanted to make. Making it well. Making it with care. [thoughtful pause] The Muppets weren't successful because they were designed to appeal to the broadest possible audience. They were successful because they were specific. Because they had personality. Because we cared about them enough to make them real.
C+W: What about the production values on YouTube? Some channels have film-quality budgets now—
JIM: Production value is wonderful when it serves the idea. [picks up nearby Kermit puppet, turns it in hands] This is made of foam rubber and felt. Cost maybe $3.50 in materials. But we made people believe he was alive. We gave him character. Gave him heart. [sets puppet down gently] You can have a million-dollar budget and make something hollow. Or you can have $20 and make something people remember forever. Craft isn't about money. It's about caring.
C+W: There's a whole sub-community on YouTube dedicated to practical effects and puppetry education—
JIM: [delighted] I know! I've watched some of those tutorials. People teaching how to build mechanisms. How to perform puppets for camera. How to create characters from scratch. That's the best thing about YouTube—the knowledge sharing. When I was learning puppetry, there were no tutorials. You had to figure it out. [smiles] Now someone in Bangladesh can watch a video and learn techniques that took me years to discover. That's remarkable.
C+W: What would you tell creators who want to make genuinely creative content but feel pressured to optimize for the algorithm?
JIM: [direct, kind] Make what excites you. Make what scares you a little. Make things that don't exist yet. The algorithm will figure itself out. [pause] But also understand that craft takes time. Sesame Street didn't become Sesame Street overnight. We spent months developing characters. Testing ideas. Failing. Trying again. If you're making something every week because the algorithm demands it, you don't have time for that development. You don't have time to craft.
C+W: So YouTube is fundamentally incompatible with deep creative work?
JIM: [shakes head] No. But it requires discipline. Requires knowing when to ignore the metrics. When to take three months to make something special instead of churning out weekly content. [soft smile] The platform will tell you to optimize. The algorithm will tell you to be consistent. Your craft will tell you something different. Listen to the craft. The rest will follow. Or it won't. But at least you'll have made something worth making.
C+W: If you were starting today—
JIM: [immediate, gentle] I'd make puppets. I'd put them on YouTube. I'd upload whenever something was finished, not whenever the calendar said to. I'd ignore the comments telling me it was weird. I'd keep making things until someone noticed. [picks up Kermit again] Because good work finds its audience eventually. Not always quickly. Not always easily. But it finds them. [makes Kermit wave] Hi-ho, algorithm. We're doing this our way.

ANTHONY BOURDAIN ON FOOD YOUTUBE

SATIRICAL / FICTIONAL — Anthony Bourdain (1956-2018) did not participate in this Q&A.
C+W: Tony, thanks for sitting down with us—
BOURDAIN: [lights cigarette] This better not be about mukbang.
C+W: We wanted to talk about food storytelling on YouTube—
BOURDAIN: [exhales smoke] Good. Because mukbang is a fucking crime against food. Watching someone eat 50 pounds of crawfish in silence while staring at a camera is not food content. It's a cry for help with production values.
C+W: [laughs] Fair. But YouTube has created some genuinely good food storytelling. Channels like Eater, First We Feast—
BOURDAIN: Eater's good. They understand context. They show the people behind the food. The culture. The history. That's what food media should be. [pause] But for every Eater, there's 50 channels doing "I ate at every Michelin star restaurant in Tokyo" where the focus is on the creator's reaction face, not the fucking food. Not the chef who spent 40 years perfecting a technique. Just some kid going "WOW THIS IS AMAZING" in Times Square voice.
C+W: The personality-driven approach has definitely taken over—
BOURDAIN: Because the platform rewards it. [taps ash] Look, I get it. I was a personality too. But the food was always the star. The people making the food were the stars. I was just the asshole with a camera and a plane ticket asking questions. On YouTube, the creator IS the star. The food is the prop. That's backwards.
C+W: There are exceptions. Channels focused on technique, on regional cuisines—
BOURDAIN: Yeah, there are. [softer] I've watched some incredible stuff. That Pasta Grannies channel? Fucking beautiful. Just old Italian women making pasta the way they've made it for 60 years. No gimmicks. No bullshit. No "SMASH THAT SUBSCRIBE BUTTON." Just craft. [pause] That's what food media should be. Showing people who've dedicated their lives to making one thing exceptionally well. Letting them tell their own stories.
C+W: But those channels struggle compared to the high-energy, fast-cut style—
BOURDAIN: Of course they do. Because watching an 80-year-old woman roll out pasta dough doesn't trigger the dopamine response that YouTube's algorithm is designed to exploit. [leans forward] The algorithm wants you watching longer. Clicking more. Staying engaged. You know what's engaging? Watching someone eat a burger that's bigger than their head. You know what's not engaging according to the algorithm? Watching someone braise short ribs for six hours. But which one is craft?
C+W: You've talked before about the importance of context in food media—
BOURDAIN: Food without context is just calories. [emphatic] When you eat pho in Hanoi, you're not just eating soup. You're eating history. Colonial occupation. Immigration. Family recipes passed down through war and displacement. That's the story. YouTube food channels that just show the eating? They're missing the entire point. [pause] Though I will say, some channels get it. That guy who travels to different countries and just eats street food with locals? He gets it. The food is the entry point. The connection is the content.
C+W: What about the technical cooking channels? Kenji López-Alt, Adam Ragusea, Ethan Chlebowski—
BOURDAIN: Kenji's great. He's actually teaching people to understand cooking. Not just follow recipes. Understand why things work. Why you salt the water. Why you rest the meat. That's craft. [takes drag] Ragusea I have mixed feelings about. He's smart. He explains things well. But sometimes he's too focused on optimization. "This is easier." "This is faster." Okay, but is it better? Craft isn't always about the fastest path.
C+W: There's a trend toward "restaurant-quality at home" content—
BOURDAIN: [groans] Yeah, I've seen that. "Make Michelin-star food in your home kitchen!" It's bullshit. You can't. You don't have the equipment. You don't have the ingredients. You don't have the training. [softens slightly] But more than that—why would you want to? Home cooking should be home cooking. It should be delicious, satisfying, achievable. Trying to replicate restaurant food at home misses what makes restaurant food special AND what makes home cooking special.
C+W: What would you tell someone who wants to make food content that actually matters?
BOURDAIN: [stubs out cigarette, direct eye contact] Tell stories. Real ones. Not "I tried every fast food chain" stories. Find people who give a shit about food. Who've dedicated their lives to it. Let them talk. Don't interrupt with your personality or your opinions. [pause] Show context. Show culture. Show the hands making the food. And for fuck's sake, turn off the background music and let people hear the sounds of cooking. The sizzle. The knife on the board. That's the real ASMR.
C+W: If you were starting in food media today, would you use YouTube?
BOURDAIN: [long pause] Maybe. As a tool, not as a destination. I'd use it to share the stories I wanted to tell, but I wouldn't let it dictate what stories I told or how I told them. [lights another cigarette] The moment you start making content decisions based on analytics, you've lost. You're not a storyteller anymore. You're a content farmer. And I didn't spend 40 years in kitchens to become a fucking content farmer.

ROGER EBERT ON VIDEO CRITICISM

SATIRICAL / FICTIONAL — Roger Ebert (1942-2013) did not participate in this Q&A.
C+W: Roger, YouTube has created an entire generation of film and media critics—
EBERT: [slight smile] Has it? Or has it created an entire generation of people with opinions and a microphone?
C+W: You don't think YouTube criticism is legitimate?
EBERT: I didn't say that. [thoughtful] Some of the best film criticism I've ever read—or watched—has been on YouTube. But the vast majority isn't criticism. It's reaction. "Here's what I thought about the new Marvel movie" isn't criticism. It's reporting your emotional response. Criticism requires analysis. Understanding of craft. Historical context. A perspective earned through years of watching, reading, thinking.
C+W: There are channels that do deep analysis. Every Frame a Painting—
EBERT: [immediate engagement] Tony Zhou's work is exceptional. That's exactly what I mean. He doesn't just tell you whether he liked something. He shows you how it works. How Kurosawa moves his camera. How Edgar Wright uses editing. How the Coen Brothers frame shots. [leans forward] That's criticism. That's craft. He's teaching people to see. Not just watch. See.
C+W: But he stopped making videos years ago—
EBERT: [nods knowingly] Because that level of criticism takes time. Each of his videos required watching dozens of films. Studying techniques. Crafting the essay. Editing it precisely. [pause] You can't do that on a weekly schedule. You can't do that while chasing the algorithm. Real criticism requires space to think. To develop ideas. To be wrong and revise. YouTube doesn't reward that. It rewards volume.
C+W: There are successful critics who do maintain schedules. RedLetterMedia, YourMovieSucks—
EBERT: RedLetterMedia is interesting. [considering] Their Plinkett reviews are legitimately brilliant criticism disguised as comedy. They understand narrative structure. They understand character. They can articulate why something doesn't work on a fundamental level. [pause] But they've also had to dilute that with react content and quick reviews because that's what the platform demands. Even they can't just make the long, thoughtful stuff anymore.
C+W: What about the whole "video essay" format that YouTube has popularized?
EBERT: It has potential. [measured] The best video essays use the medium properly—they show you what they're talking about. Film criticism in print requires describing what's on screen. Video criticism can just... show it. That's powerful. [slight frown] But most video essays are just someone reading their blog post aloud over movie clips. That's not using the medium. That's laziness with production value.
C+W: There's been a proliferation of "why X is brilliant" videos—
EBERT: [slight grimace] Yes. The positive-only criticism trend. [pause] Look, I understand the impulse. People want to share things they love. That's admirable. But criticism that only celebrates is incomplete. The word "critic" comes from the Greek "kritikos"—able to discern. That requires judgment. Sometimes negative judgment. [leans back] When every video essay is about why something is brilliant or genius or underrated, the words lose meaning. Craft criticism requires making distinctions. Saying this works, this doesn't, and here's why.
C+W: But negative criticism gets accused of being clickbait now—
EBERT: Sometimes it is. [frank] "Why [Popular Thing] is Actually Garbage" is clickbait. It's performative contrarianism. But "Here are the specific ways this film's structure undermines its themes" is criticism. The difference is specificity. Precision. Evidence. [pause] Good negative criticism isn't about being mean. It's about holding work to high standards and explaining when it falls short. That's necessary. That's how art improves.
C+W: YouTube has definitely changed how people consume criticism. They want personality, production value—
EBERT: They want entertainment. [not dismissive, just factual] And that's fine. Criticism can be entertaining. Siskel and I proved that. We were entertaining! But we were entertaining and substantive. We had actual disagreements based on actual critical frameworks. [slight smile] YouTube criticism often prioritizes the entertainment over the substance. The personality over the perspective. You remember the creator, not the argument.
C+W: What makes good YouTube film criticism?
EBERT: [thoughtful pause] The same things that make good print criticism. A clear perspective earned through extensive viewing. The ability to articulate how something works, not just whether you liked it. Historical and cultural context. And most importantly— [emphasis] —the humility to know that your opinion is just that. An opinion. Not truth. Not law. [softer] The best critics make you want to see the work yourself. To form your own opinion. Bad critics tell you what to think. Good critics teach you how to think.
C+W: If you were starting as a critic today, would you do it on YouTube?
EBERT: [considers carefully] I'd use it. But I wouldn't let it use me. [pause] I'd make the videos I wanted to make, on the schedule that served the work. If that meant one video a month of deep analysis instead of weekly reactions, so be it. Quality over quantity. Always. [slight smile] And I'd turn off the analytics. The number of views shouldn't influence whether your criticism is good. The quality of the argument should. That's craft. That's criticism. That's the job.

EVERY FRAME A PAINTING (ARCHIVE REVIEW)

Creator: Tony Zhou | Subscribers: 3.5M | Active: 2014-2016

Let's talk about a channel that hasn't uploaded in eight years and is still required viewing for anyone who wants to understand visual storytelling. Let's talk about the channel that invented the modern video essay format. Let's talk about Every Frame a Painting.

Tony Zhou created 28 videos between 2014 and 2016. Then he stopped. No goodbye video. No explanation. Just... finished. Those 28 videos have been watched over 100 million times. They're taught in film schools. They're referenced in other video essays so often that Zhou deserves residuals. They changed how people think about movies.

This is craft at its absolute peak.

Every Frame a Painting did something simple: it showed you how movies work. Not in a theoretical way. Not in a film-student-trying-to-sound-smart way. In a "here is exactly what the director did and here is exactly why it works" way. The video on Jackie Chan shows how action scenes are choreographed for clarity. The video on Kurosawa shows how movement creates meaning. The video on the Marvel Cinematic Universe exposes how safe, unremarkable filmmaking has become normalized.

"Zhou didn't tell you what to think. He showed you how to see."

The craft is visible in every second. Zhou didn't just grab clips and talk over them. He edited them to make a point. He isolated specific techniques. He showed the same scene multiple times, each time highlighting a different element—the editing rhythm, the sound design, the camera movement. He used music carefully. He varied his pacing to match the content. When discussing slow, deliberate filmmaking (Terrence Malick), the video itself slowed down. When discussing kinetic action (Edgar Wright), the editing accelerated.

This is what it means to use the medium properly. Zhou wasn't making video essays because video was trendy. He was making video essays because the points he wanted to make required video. You couldn't explain Buster Keaton's visual comedy in text. You had to show the frames. You had to show the timing. The medium was the message.

The production quality was exceptional but not flashy. Clean editing. Clear audio. No motion graphics beyond simple text overlays. The focus was always on the film clips and the analysis. Zhou's narration was measured, informative, occasionally dry-humorous, but never performative. He wasn't a personality. He was a teacher.

What makes Every Frame a Painting essential—what makes it craft—is the depth of understanding behind each video. Zhou wasn't sharing hot takes. He was sharing insights earned through deep study. The video on David Fincher's invisible editing reveals techniques that most viewers had never consciously noticed but had definitely felt. The video on ensemble staging in The Fugitive shows how a competent director (Andrew Davis) creates clarity in complex action scenes. These aren't surface-level observations. This is film theory made accessible without being dumbed down.

The channel's legacy is complicated. On one hand, it inspired thousands of people to make video essays. On the other hand, most of those video essays missed the point. They copied the format—narration over clips—but not the rigor. They copied the style but not the substance. Zhou showed filmmaking techniques with precision and specificity. His imitators often just... share opinions about movies with clips in the background.

Zhou stopped making videos because, reportedly, the level of research and work required wasn't sustainable. Each video took weeks of watching films, studying techniques, crafting the essay, editing it precisely. You can't do that while holding down a professional film editing career. You can't do that on a weekly schedule. So he stopped. And that decision—to stop rather than dilute the work—is itself a statement about craft.

Quality over quantity. Always.

PRIMITIVE TECHNOLOGY

Creator: John Plant | Subscribers: 10.5M | Est. 2015

John Plant makes fires. He makes pots. He makes shelters. He makes tools. He does this in the Australian bush with no modern equipment, no narration, and no explanation beyond subtitles. His videos average 10-15 minutes. They regularly get 5-10 million views. He is YouTube's most elegant argument for craft over content.

Primitive Technology is minimalism as artform. Each video shows Plant creating something using only natural materials and techniques humans developed thousands of years ago. Fire from sticks. Clay pottery fired in a ground kiln. Water-powered hammers. Charcoal furnaces. Tiled roofs. The process is methodical. Plant gathers materials. Prepares them. Constructs carefully. Tests. Adjusts. Completes. The camera is static. The editing is minimal. There is no music. Just the sounds of the forest and Plant working.

This is craft stripped to its absolute essence.

"Plant's genius is showing that technique itself is the content. The process IS the point."

The videos work because they're hypnotic. Watching Plant weave a basket from vines or shape a stone axe head engages something primal. It's satisfying in a way that's hard to articulate. Part of it is the competence—Plant clearly knows what he's doing. Part of it is the patience—nothing is rushed. Part of it is the authenticity—this is real skill demonstrated in real time. But mostly it's that the videos respect the craft enough to just... show it. No hype. No personality. No "make sure to like and subscribe." Just a man making things with his hands.

The production approach is deliberately simple. Single camera angle, usually wide enough to show the full work area. Natural lighting. No cuts except for time-lapses when processes take hours. The subtitles (which are optional—you have to turn them on) explain what's happening but don't editorialize. "Making charcoal from wood." "Fired to 900 degrees." "Will use this to make iron." Purely informational.

Plant uploads irregularly. Sometimes monthly. Sometimes not for years. He doesn't explain absences. He doesn't apologize for gaps. The videos appear when they appear. This approach would kill most YouTube channels. For Primitive Technology, it reinforces the authenticity. These aren't content calendar items. These are projects. They're done when they're done.

The craft on display is multilayered. Obviously there's the physical craft—the actual skills of primitive technology. But there's also the craft of teaching those skills through video. Plant's camera placement shows exactly what you need to see. The pacing lets you understand each step. The demonstration is clear without being didactic. You could actually learn from these videos. People have learned from these videos. There's a whole community of people doing primitive technology projects inspired by Plant's work.

What separates Primitive Technology from imitators is the authenticity. Plant isn't performing primitive technology. He's doing primitive technology. The videos aren't optimized for retention or engagement. They're optimized for showing the process accurately. When something takes eight hours, Plant shows enough of those eight hours that you understand the time investment. When something requires precision, the camera holds on the detail long enough for you to see the precision.

The channel has spawned countless imitators, most of them terrible. Channels with narration explaining everything. Channels with upbeat music. Channels with multiple camera angles and fast cuts. Channels where the "primitive" technology mysteriously produces perfect results with suspiciously modern-looking joins. These channels miss the point entirely. Primitive Technology works because it's unadorned. Because it trusts that the craft itself is interesting enough.

After a two-year absence, Plant returned with a video about water-powered hammers. No explanation for the gap. No "sorry I've been gone." Just: here's a water-powered hammer I made. The video did 8 million views in a week. Quality endures. Craft finds its audience. Patience is rewarded.

5-MINUTE CRAFTS

Creator: TheSoul Publishing (Content Farm) | Subscribers: 80M | Est. 2016

Let's talk about the anti-craft. Let's talk about what happens when you optimize content production to the point that the actual making of things becomes irrelevant. Let's talk about 5-Minute Crafts, the most-subscribed craft channel on YouTube, and one of the most cynical content operations on the platform.

5-Minute Crafts is not made by craftspeople. It's made by a content farm called TheSoul Publishing that runs over 100 YouTube channels producing algorithmic slop at industrial scale. The channel posts multiple videos per day. Each video shows 10-20 "crafts" or "hacks" in rapid succession. None of them work. Most of them are actively dangerous. All of them are designed to generate views through the sheer velocity of absurdity.

This is the opposite of everything we've covered in this issue.

"5-Minute Crafts is to craft what spam is to cuisine."

The videos follow a formula. Quick cuts. Bright colors. Hands doing things. No narration. Upbeat royalty-free music. The "crafts" are things like: use a hot glue gun to make a phone case. Use toothpaste to clean your TV screen (do not do this). Use a balloon and concrete to make a bowl (it will explode). Use nail polish and water to make marbled paper (maybe works, probably doesn't). The production style is designed for maximum scroll-stopping power on mobile feeds. Bright. Fast. Tactile.

None of this is about craft. This is content as industrial product. The "crafts" aren't tested. They're not refined. They're barely even attempted beyond what you see on screen. Multiple investigations by actual crafters have shown that most 5-Minute Crafts projects are physically impossible, wasteful, dangerous, or solved better by just buying the actual product you're supposedly "making."

The channel exists because it exploits several algorithmic vulnerabilities. First, the high posting frequency games YouTube's recommendation system. Second, the non-verbal format is internationally accessible. Third, the rapid-fire format maximizes watch time—even if you hate most of the "crafts," you keep watching to see the next one. Fourth, the tactile, hands-on content is satisfying to watch even when you know it's nonsense. It's the visual equivalent of empty calories.

What makes 5-Minute Crafts actively harmful is that it poses as education. People—often children—watch these videos and attempt the projects. They waste materials. They damage their possessions. They sometimes hurt themselves. The comment sections are full of people reporting failures. The channel doesn't care. It's not accountable to results. It's accountable to metrics.

There's also the broader cultural damage. 5-Minute Crafts teaches that craft is about speed and hacks rather than skill and patience. That you can solve any problem with hot glue and random household objects. That the process doesn't matter, only the appearance of having made something. This is the exact opposite of the craft ethos. It's craft as performance. As content. As lie.

The production values, ironically, are competent. The videos are well-lit. The hands are manicured. The editing is tight. The pacing is energetic. If this much production effort went into actually good craft content, it could be valuable. But TheSoul Publishing doesn't care about value. They care about volume. They've industrialized content creation to the point where quality is irrelevant.

Other craft YouTubers despise 5-Minute Crafts. Channels like Ann Reardon (How to Cook That) have dedicated entire series to debunking their dangerous "hacks." Every actual crafter who encounters the channel has the same reaction: horror that this represents "craft" to millions of people. That this is what the algorithm rewards. That this is what wins.

5-Minute Crafts has 80 million subscribers. Technology Connections has 3 million. Primitive Technology has 10 million. The algorithm doesn't reward craft. It rewards volume, frequency, and algorithmic optimization. 5-Minute Crafts is the logical endpoint of that system. It's what happens when content becomes completely divorced from making anything real.

BOSS FIGHT

Head-to-head battles. One winner. No draws.

PRIMITIVE TECHNOLOGY vs. HOW TO MAKE EVERYTHING

Both channels are about making things from scratch. Both show genuine craft. Both have educational value. Both reject the standard YouTube personality-driven format. But only one can win.

PRIMITIVE TECHNOLOGY

Style: Silent demonstration. No narration. Pure technique.

Approach: Ancient human technologies recreated authentically in the Australian bush.

Signature Move: The absolute minimalism. Just a man, materials, and fire.

HOW TO MAKE EVERYTHING

Style: Narrated journey. Personality-driven. Educational focus.

Approach: Modern person learning to make modern things from scratch, showing the full supply chain.

Signature Move: "From scratch" means FROM SCRATCH. Making a sandwich starting with planting wheat.

ROUND 1: AUTHENTICITY

Primitive Technology: Plant is doing the actual thing. He's not recreating primitive technology for content—he's practicing primitive technology and filming it. Every technique is period-accurate. Every material is sourced on-site. Zero shortcuts. Zero modern tools beyond the camera.

How To Make Everything: Andy George is explicitly a modern person learning these skills. He consults experts. He uses modern knowledge to recreate historical processes. He'll use power tools to make historical tools. The project is authentic in spirit but not in execution.

WINNER: PRIMITIVE TECHNOLOGY — This isn't even close. Plant's authenticity is total.

ROUND 2: EDUCATIONAL VALUE

Primitive Technology: You watch someone make fire or pottery. You understand the process. But you're not getting explanation. No context on why this technique versus that technique. Limited educational depth beyond "this is how it's done."

How To Make Everything: George explains everything. The history. The chemistry. The why. When he makes a sandwich from scratch, you learn about agriculture, milling, fermentation, animal husbandry. The educational scope is vast.

WINNER: HOW TO MAKE EVERYTHING — If you want to actually learn about processes and systems, George is your guy.

ROUND 3: CRAFT ON DISPLAY

Primitive Technology: Every video is pure craft. You're watching genuine skill. Plant knows what he's doing. The competence is meditative. The technique is refined through practice.

How To Make Everything: George is learning on camera. The craft is in the journey, not the execution. He makes mistakes. He consults experts. He shows the learning process. This is valuable but it's not masterful craft.

WINNER: PRIMITIVE TECHNOLOGY — Watching a master work is different than watching someone learn. Both valuable. One is craft.

ROUND 4: PRODUCTION VALUES

Primitive Technology: One camera. Static shots. Natural audio. Minimal editing. The production serves the content with zero flash.

How To Make Everything: Multiple cameras. B-roll. Graphics. Music. Editing that creates narrative momentum. The production is competent and engaging.

WINNER: HOW TO MAKE EVERYTHING — Higher production value as traditionally measured. Though one could argue Primitive Technology's minimalism is itself a production choice.

ROUND 5: REWATCHABILITY

Primitive Technology: Hypnotic. Meditative. People rewatch for relaxation. The videos work as background or focused viewing. Timeless quality.

How To Make Everything: Interesting once. Educational value persists. But once you've learned how to make a suit from scratch, do you rewatch it? Maybe for specific information, but not for the experience.

WINNER: PRIMITIVE TECHNOLOGY — The replay value is substantially higher. These are meditations, not just lessons.

WINNER: PRIMITIVE TECHNOLOGY
3 ROUNDS TO 2

EDITORIAL DECISION: This was closer than the score suggests. How To Make Everything is excellent. George is doing important educational work showing how complex supply chains really are. His "sandwich from scratch" video should be shown in schools.

But Primitive Technology represents craft in its purest form. Plant isn't explaining craft. He's not teaching craft. He's doing craft and letting you watch. That's fundamentally more powerful than any amount of educational narration. The silence is the point. The minimalism is the point. The respect for the viewer's intelligence is the point.

How To Make Everything is better if you want to learn. Primitive Technology is better if you want to witness craft. Since this issue is about craft, Primitive Technology wins.

But watch both. Seriously. Both channels are essential.

CLICKSPRING (900K subscribers)

Wait, 900K isn't under 10K! Correct. But we're breaking our own rule because Clickspring is the single best example of craft on YouTube and if you haven't seen it, you need to immediately.

Chris makes clocks. From scratch. Starting with raw brass and steel. The videos show every step of traditional clockmaking—filing, turning, polishing, assembly. The production is immaculate. The narration is calm and educational. The craftsmanship is museum-quality. This is meditation as engineering.

His ongoing project: recreating the Antikythera Mechanism, a 2,000-year-old Greek astronomical calculator, using only tools and techniques available in ancient Greece. This isn't content. This is historical research. This is art.

Start with: "Making the Planetarium - Part 1"

LETTER #1: Does expensive gear matter for craft?
"Dear CTRL+WATCH, I want to start making woodworking videos but I only have basic tools. All the successful channels seem to have thousands of dollars in equipment. Does gear actually matter or is that just YouTube flex? — Mark, Leeds"
YOB SAYS: Listen Mark, Yob's gonna be straight with you. Gear matters. But not the way you think. A master craftsman with basic tools will make better work than a beginner with a full workshop. The gear doesn't make the craft. The craft makes the craft.
That said, you need GOOD basic tools. Not expensive. Good. A sharp chisel is more important than an expensive chisel. A flat workbench matters more than a fancy workbench. Start with the fundamentals. Make things. Show the process. When you actually need better tools because the basic ones are limiting your work, you'll know. Until then, stop making excuses and start making sawdust.
